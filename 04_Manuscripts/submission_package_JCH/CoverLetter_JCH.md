We are pleased to submit our manuscript entitled "Association between Topographic Slope, Urban Walkability, and Diabetes Management Indicators: An Ecological Study of 47 Japanese Prefectures" for consideration for publication in the *Journal of Community Health*.

**Summary**

This ecological study investigates the associations between topographic slope, urban walkability, and four diabetes-related indicators (HbA1c, BMI obesity rate, waist circumference, triglycerides) across all 47 Japanese prefectures, using data from the National Database of Health Insurance Claims and Specific Health Checkups (NDB) Open Data for fiscal year 2023. We applied ordinary least squares (OLS) regression adjusted for aging rate and income per capita, with conditional spatial lag models (SLM) estimated when significant spatial autocorrelation was detected.

Our principal findings are: (1) urban walkability was consistently and protectively associated with all diabetes indicators; (2) topographic slope showed limited direct associations, suggesting that urban design matters more than natural terrain for population-level metabolic health; and (3) BMI obesity exhibited strong spatial spillover effects (spatial lag coefficient rho = 0.753, *p* < 0.001), absent in HbA1c (Moran's I = −0.072, *p* = 0.638). The conditional spatial regression approach revealed that ignoring spatial autocorrelation in obesity data severely underestimates explanatory power (R² = 0.437 → Pseudo R² = 0.784).

**Relevance to the Journal of Community Health**

We believe this manuscript is well suited to the *Journal of Community Health* because:

1. It addresses a core community health question—how built environments and physical geography shape metabolic disease burdens at the population level.
2. It provides novel evidence that obesity prevention requires inter-prefectural coordination (spatial spillover rho = 0.753), while diabetes management can remain locally focused—a distinction with direct policy implications for Health Japan 21 (Third Term, 2024–2035).
3. The nationwide Japanese dataset (N = 47 prefectures, complete coverage) extends the built environment–diabetes literature beyond predominantly Western contexts.
4. The outcome-specific application of spatial regression models offers methodological guidance for community health researchers.
